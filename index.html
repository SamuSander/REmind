State of Research: The Impact of Targeted Memory Reactivation (TMR) on Declarative and Emotional Learning Processes


The role of sleep in the consolidation of emotional and declarative memories has gained increasing scientific interest, particularly regarding the potential of the Targeted Memory Reactivation (TMR) approach. TMR aims to selectively reactivate specific memory content during sleep to enhance its consolidation. This approach has found potential applications in treating mental disorders associated with fear generalization, improving social biases, and mitigating negative memories. This overview summarizes the current research on TMR and its impact on declarative and emotional learning processes.


Sleep Phases and TMR

Sleep is divided into REM (Rapid Eye Movement) and non-REM phases, with the latter further divided into light sleep (NonREM1 and NonREM2) and deep sleep (NonREM3, also known as Slow-Wave Sleep, SWS). While REM phases are mostly associated with dreaming, SWS is crucial for memory consolidation, supported by synchronized neural firing sequences that enhance memory consolidation.


Neurobiological Foundations and Mechanisms

The hippocampal region is central in modulating fear expression and differentiating between threats and safety. The dentate gyrus plays a key role in forming new memory representations to minimize overlap with previous memories. TMR during SWS has successfully activated brain-associated regions, including the left hippocampus and right parahippocampal cortex, highlighting their importance in memory reactivation.


Olfactory Cues as an Opportunity

TMR offers unique opportunities for the selective manipulation and enhancement of specific memory contents using auditory or olfactory cues. Olfactory cues, which are transmitted directly from the olfactory bulb to the hippocampus and amygdala, are particularly effective in reactivating declarative and emotional memory content.
